Minimal Creative Portfolio

The pension data sample management database is part of the whole pension project I have been working on at Health and Retirement Study in Institute for Social Research. Since the project contains sensitive confidential data, my main focus will be showing how design thinking helped me and the team better utilize the data collecting process and the database management.

How to Obtain Precise Information Completely and Efficiently

One of the main research topics Health and Retirement Study (HRS) Pension project focused on was to compare self-report data from national survey results versus calculated data in order to test certain estimation based on several economic models. On the one hand, the survey data was developed and collected through our Survey Research Center, on the other hand, the calculated pension data was obtained by our team. In this sense, the sample data collecting process became extremely important. Therefore, a highly standardized work process of selecting useful information and tracking progress was in need.

My supervisor and I were in charge of building the sample management database and designing the user interface. We needed to design a database that contains several screens for analysts to work along the whole data collecting process. Since this is a more 20 years longitudinal project, there existed certain rules and standards in organizing the data collecting process. However, certain standards and designs no longer apply to the current database structure, therefore, we made a new database plus better user interactions.

Challenge and Solutions

Figuring out the most efficient workflow and determining the priority of information is the largest challenge in constructing the database. There needs the implementation of design thinking. In order to tackle down the problem, we worked as the following steps:

1. Collaborate with Analysts that will work directly with the database

I first worked with one analyst to go through the entire sample data collecting process, which includes acquiring employer information for respondents, finding potential benefit plans, reviewing respondent eligibility, assigning benefit plans for respondents, building mathematical models, testing and reviewing. At each step, I recorded information that was extracted from survey and information that needs to be acquired. The information then became different variables and fields for analysts to input or output.

2. Separate Screens to realize different goals

Since the sample data collecting process is not easy and is not achieved by one analyst, we decided to make separate screens to realize different goal so that it will be easier to test and conduct data quality checks. We also build separate screens for each analyst, so analysts can either work at different paces or collaborate to finish one screen.

3. Iterative Design and Tests

For each screen I built, I let analysts test out and raise up any questions or suggestions they have. With several iterations during the design process and by working closely with all analysts in the team, we managed to launch the database when new cohort survey data came in and analysts could work with it right away.

4. Design Each Screen to be Simple, Inclusive and Functional

In terms of the style, we did not apply visually complicated design, instead, the team agreed that the database served primarily as a database that stores data, manages information structure and monitors working progress. It would be excessive and distracting to add extra visual elements.
